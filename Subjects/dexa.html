Anatomy Overview

Lumbar spine anatomy illustration would appear here

The lumbar spine consists of five vertebrae (L1-L5) located in the lower back. DEXA scanning of this area is crucial for diagnosing osteoporosis and monitoring treatment efficacy. Proper patient positioning is essential for accurate results.

Common Indications

  • Postmenopausal women
  • Long-term steroid use
  • Previous fragility fracture
  • Monitoring osteoporosis treatment

Technical Factors

  • Dual-energy X-ray source
  • Low radiation dose (1-10 μSv)
  • Scan time: 1-5 minutes
  • High resolution detection

Patient Preparation

  • No calcium supplements 24h prior
  • Wear metal-free clothing
  • Remove jewelry/zippers
  • Report recent contrast studies

Positioning Protocol

  1. Patient positioning: Supine on scanning table with hips and knees flexed using positioning block
  2. Spine alignment: Ensure spine is straight and centered on table
  3. Arms placement: Positioned away from scanning field
  4. Scan area: From L1 to L5 vertebrae
  5. Analysis: Automated ROI placement with technologist verification

Common Challenges

  • Scoliosis or spinal deformity
  • Osteoarthritis with osteophytes
  • Previous spinal surgery
  • Patient movement during scan

Quality Control

  • Daily calibration checks
  • Spine phantom scanning
  • Regular service maintenance
  • Technologist competency assessment

T-Score Interpretation

T-Score Range Classification Clinical Significance
-1.0 and above Normal Normal bone density
-1.0 to -2.5 Osteopenia Low bone mass
-2.5 and below Osteoporosis High fracture risk
-2.5 and below with fracture Severe Osteoporosis Established bone fragility

Z-Score Interpretation

Z-Score Range Interpretation
-2.0 and above Within expected range for age
Below -2.0 Lower than expected for age

Clinical Applications

Diagnostic Criteria

  • WHO classification system
  • Lowest T-score at hip or spine
  • Consider clinical risk factors
  • FRAX® algorithm integration

Monitoring Changes

  • Minimum 1-2 years between scans
  • LSC (Least Significant Change) concept
  • Same machine preferred for follow-up
  • Consider biological variability

Reporting

  • Include T-scores and Z-scores
  • Reference database used
  • Technical quality assessment
  • Clinical correlation advice

FRAX® Assessment

The FRAX® tool integrates bone density results with clinical risk factors to estimate 10-year probability of major osteoporotic fracture.
